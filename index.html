Sponsor co-branding · Design rationale

Making the Wiom logo belong on the YUCA 2026 poster

Wiom presents the 14th Young Uttarakhand Cine Awards — but on the current poster, the logo fights the artwork instead of headlining it. One hard constraint: nothing else on the poster can change. Below is the diagnosis, three placement options, a clear recommendation, and the full reasoning behind the call.

Event: YUCA 2026 · 30 Aug · Bharat Mandapam Role: Presenting sponsor Constraint: poster art is locked
01

Why the current placement looks odd

“Odd” is fixable only once it's named precisely. It isn't one problem — it's four, stacked. None of them are the logo's fault: white-on-pink is Wiom's correct primary lockup. It's simply deployed into a poster that already has its own visual system.

  1. 1

    Magenta vibrates against crimson

    Wiom pink (#D9008D) and the poster's deep reds are neighbouring hues at high saturation. Neighbours don't contrast cleanly (the way gold and white do here) and don't harmonise either — they shimmer against each other. That shimmer is most of what reads as “odd”.

  2. 2

    The heaviest color block on the poster

    The solid pink slab is the largest single flat-color area on the entire artwork — visually heavier than the event title itself. The eye lands on the box before the event name, which inverts the hierarchy a presenting sponsor should respect.

  3. 3

    It breaks the poster's own logo rule

    Every other partner on this poster — BAUX, Uttarakhand Tourism, 99 Online, Oho Radio, Young Uttarakhand — sits in a white rounded container. Five out of five. Wiom is the only logo that ignores the convention, which is exactly why it looks pasted on.

  4. 4

    Sticker effect

    A hard-edged solid fill sits flat on top of the sparkle-textured background with no mediating edge. Everything else on the poster either glows, blends, or lives inside the shared white-chip system — the pink box does none of the three.

The core insight: because the poster can't change, the logo must adapt to the poster's system — not the other way around. That single constraint drives every recommendation on this page.
02

Reading the poster's own rules first

Before proposing anything, I audited the locked artwork the way I'd audit a design system — extracting its palette and the conventions it already enforces. The answer to “how should a logo appear here?” is written on the poster itself.

Deep crimson≈ #400710 · ground
Maroon glow≈ #7E1220 · light areas
Rich gold≈ #F0C24B · event voice
Warm white≈ #FFF6E6 · support text
Trophy black≈ #161313 · anchors
Wiom pink#D9008D · the outsider — nothing on the poster anticipates it
Convention 1

Partner logos live in white rounded chips. White is the poster's designated neutral stage for third-party marks — it lets each brand keep its own colors without touching the red.

Evidence: BAUX, Uttarakhand Tourism, 99 Online, Oho Radio, YU — 5 of 5 existing logos.

Convention 2

Only the event's own voice sits bare on the background — the title in gold, “PRESENTS”, the date, “Join us” in white/gold script. Bare placement signals “this is the event speaking”.

Evidence: every unboxed element on the poster is event copy, not a brand.

Convention 3

Color encodes hierarchy: gold = the event itself, warm white = connective text, white chips = partners. A saturated brand-colored slab has no assigned role in this language.

Evidence: the only saturated non-red block on the poster is… the Wiom box.

03

Three ways to place the logo

All three keep the logo in its existing slot — top-centre, above “PRESENTS” — because position is the one thing the current version gets right. What changes is the variant, container and scale.

Recommended

A · White chip, brand-pink logo

Use Wiom's standard on-white lockup — pink mark, wordmark and tagline — inside a white rounded container that matches the poster's existing chip system.

  • It joins the poster's own convention. White chips are how this artwork presents partners; Wiom stops being the one exception.
  • Brand color survives at 100%. #D9008D on white is an official Wiom variant — no recoloring, no compromise. Pink is actually more legible here than white-on-pink was on red.
  • Hierarchy restores itself. A white chip weighs roughly half of a solid pink slab at the same footprint, so the gold title takes back the first glance.
  • Zero approval risk. Every mature brand ships an on-white lockup; nothing new needs designing or signing off.
  • Prominence comes from position and size — top-centre, above “PRESENTS”, sized a step larger than the BAUX / Tourism chips. Presenting-sponsor status stays unmistakable.

05

How I approached it — and how the call was made

The full reasoning, step by step, so the recommendation can be challenged on its logic rather than taken on faith.

Start from the constraint, not the logo

“The rest of the poster cannot change” eliminates 90% of the usual moves — recoloring the background, rebalancing the layout, moving other elements. The only levers left are Wiom-side: which logo variant we use, whether it sits in a container, and at what scale. Framing the levers first kept the exploration honest.

Audit the poster like a design system

I extracted the palette (crimson ground, gold event voice, warm-white support text) and looked for repeated rules. The decisive find: all five existing partner logos sit in white rounded chips. The poster already documents how a brand should appear on it — we just hadn't read the documentation.

Name the oddness precisely

“It looks odd” became four specific violations: hue vibration (magenta on crimson), weight inversion (the slab outweighs the title), convention break (the only unchipped logo), and the sticker effect (hard fill over texture). Naming them matters because each violation implies its own fix — and a proposal is only credible if it addresses all four.

Inventory Wiom's own degrees of freedom

Mature logo systems ship variants precisely for co-branding: primary (white-on-pink), on-white (pink-on-white), and reversed mono (white). Using the on-white variant is not a brand compromise — it's what that variant exists for. This reframing is what unlocked the solution space beyond “shrink the pink box”.

Score the candidates against five criteria

Brand-color fidelity, harmony with the locked artwork, hierarchy (does the title read first?), legibility at distance, and approval effort. Option A scores high on all five. Option B wins on pure integration but sacrifices the pink. Option C preserves the primary lockup but only mediates the clash — it never resolves it.

Keep the alternates alive — the real fork is governance, not design

Whether B or C is viable depends on brand rules, not aesthetics: B needs a reversed-variant approval; C exists for the case where sponsorship terms mandate the primary lockup. Presenting all three with an explicit ranking lets marketing make the governance call without re-doing the design thinking.

Sanity-check at real viewing sizes

Awards posters are met on a phone first — a 300-px WhatsApp forward — and in print second. I checked each option as a thumbnail: does the event title still land first, and does “Wiom” still read? Option A passes both at every size; that closed the decision.

One temptation deliberately rejected: recoloring the Wiom logo gold or gold-on-red to “match the poster”. It would blend beautifully — and fail completely. Gold is the event's voice on this artwork; a gold Wiom logo would read as event property, dissolve brand recognition, and violate the logo's color rules. Harmony that erases the brand isn't harmony a sponsor should buy.
06

Production checklist

✓ Do

  • Use the official on-white vector lockup — mark, wordmark and Hindi tagline in #D9008D
  • Match the white chip's warmth and corner radius to the poster's existing partner chips
  • Keep the chip at ≈ 30% of poster width, centred in the existing slot above “PRESENTS”
  • Hold clear space of at least one loop-mark height inside the chip
  • Proof at thumbnail size (320 px wide) and at print size before release
  • Get the final placement signed off by both brand and the event's design team

✕ Don't

  • Don't recolor the logo gold, red or any “matching” shade — ever
  • Don't place raw pink on the red background without a white stage between them
  • Don't add glows, gradients, bevels or drop shadows to the chip
  • Don't let the lockup outweigh the event title — sponsor frames the event, never competes with it
  • Don't stretch, condense or re-set the wordmark to fit a space
  • Don't rebuild the logo from this page's mockups — they're indicative recreations, not artwork
